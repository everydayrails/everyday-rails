Here's a list of books I like for learning Rails and Ruby&mdash;and getting better at using them as everyday parts of my toolbox as a web developer. Purchasing books from these links helps keep Everyday Rails running. Thanks for your support!

h2. Learning Ruby on Rails

h4. "Agile Web Development with Rails, Fourth Edition":http://amzn.to/n3NP4S

Sam Ruby, Dave Thomas, and David Heinemeier Hanson's _Agile Rails_, as it's commonly called, is how I first learned Ruby and Rails back in 2005. It was in its first edition back then, and Rails has evolved a great deal since that time. I've kept an eye on subsequent versions of the book, though, and still think it's among the best ways to get started with the framework.

Michael Hartl's _Ruby on Rails Tutorial_ is a thorough introduction to the entire Rails ecosystem, including version control with Git, test-driven development with RSpec, and deployment with Heroku. h2. Learning Ruby

h4. "Programming Ruby 1.9: The Pragmatic Programmers' Guide":http://amzn.to/rn1G2b

<div class="cover">
  <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1934356085/ref=as_li_ss_il?ie=UTF8&tag=everrail-20&linkCode=as2&camp=217145&creative=399369&creativeASIN=1934356085"><img border="0" src="http://ws.assoc-amazon.com/widgets/q?_encoding=UTF8&Format=_SL110_&ASIN=1934356085&MarketPlace=US&ID=AsinImage&WS=1&tag=everrail-20&ServiceVersion=20070822" ></a><img src="http://www.assoc-amazon.com/e/ir?t=&l=as2&o=1&a=1934356085&camp=217145&creative=399369" width="1" height="1" border="0" alt="" style="border:none !important; margin:0px !important;" />
</div>

The venerable "Pickaxe Book" from Dave Thomas, Chad Fowler, and Andy Hunt is a great follow-up to _Agile Rails_ or _Ruby on Rails 3 Tutorial_. It takes you through Ruby's internals and can help you understand some of the quote-unquote _magic_ that Rails is sometimes accused of performing. If you're developing for deployment on Heroku or a server with Ruby 1.9 on it (or one you're able to put Ruby 1.9 on), get this edition.

h4. "Programming Ruby: The Pragmatic Programmers' Guide, Second Edition":http://amzn.to/oWYAwN

<div class="cover">
  <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0974514055/ref=as_li_ss_il?ie=UTF8&tag=everrail-20&linkCode=as2&camp=217145&creative=399369&creativeASIN=0974514055"><img border="0" src="http://ws.assoc-amazon.com/widgets/q?_encoding=UTF8&Format=_SL110_&ASIN=0974514055&MarketPlace=US&ID=AsinImage&WS=1&tag=everrail-20&ServiceVersion=20070822" ></a><img src="http://www.assoc-amazon.com/e/ir?t=&l=as2&o=1&a=0974514055&camp=217145&creative=399369" width="1" height="1" border="0" alt="" style="border:none !important; margin:0px !important;" />
</div>

I include the second edition of _Programming Ruby_ here only because it covers Ruby 1.8 as opposed to 1.9, and many hosting providers haven't made the switch yet (Ruby Enterprise Edition is also currently based on version 1.8). If your production environment requires Ruby 1.8 this may be a good book to have on-hand&mdash;just know that the Rails community has by and large adopted 1.9 now and is moving forward with it.

h4. "Everyday Scripting with Ruby: For Teams, Testers, and You":http://amzn.to/nVLBUS

<div class="cover">
  <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/0977616614/ref=as_li_ss_il?ie=UTF8&tag=everrail-20&linkCode=as2&camp=217145&creative=399369&creativeASIN=0977616614"><img border="0" src="http://ws.assoc-amazon.com/widgets/q?_encoding=UTF8&Format=_SL110_&ASIN=0977616614&MarketPlace=US&ID=AsinImage&WS=1&tag=everrail-20&ServiceVersion=20070822" ></a><img src="http://www.assoc-amazon.com/e/ir?t=&l=as2&o=1&a=0977616614&camp=217145&creative=399369" width="1" height="1" border="0" alt="" style="border:none !important; margin:0px !important;" />
</div>

A good way to get better at Ruby is to use it outside of Rails&mdash;or any framework, for that matter. While the lessons in the Pickaxe Books aren't necessarily practical, the exercises in Brian Marick's _Everyday Scripting with Ruby_ are. Learn more about Ruby and automate some of your mundane computing tasks in the process.

h4. "Metaprogramming Ruby: Program Like the Ruby Pros":http://amzn.to/reK2Mq

<div class="cover">
  <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1934356476/ref=as_li_ss_il?ie=UTF8&tag=everrail-20&linkCode=as2&camp=217145&creative=399369&creativeASIN=1934356476"><img border="0" src="http://ws.assoc-amazon.com/widgets/q?_encoding=UTF8&Format=_SL110_&ASIN=1934356476&MarketPlace=US&ID=AsinImage&WS=1&tag=everrail-20&ServiceVersion=20070822" ></a><img src="http://www.assoc-amazon.com/e/ir?t=&l=as2&o=1&a=1934356476&camp=217145&creative=399369" width="1" height="1" border="0" alt="" style="border:none !important; margin:0px !important;" />
</div>

Metaprogramming is an awesome feature of Ruby&mdash;another key ingredient to Rails' "magic"&mdash; that can daunt developers learning the language. Paolo Perrotta's _Metaprogramming Ruby_ provides solid coverage of the concept. I don't recommend picking it up until you've got a solid grasp of Ruby, though.

h2. Testing Ruby and Rails

h4. "Rails Test Prescriptions: Keeping Your Application Healthy":http://amzn.to/ofN37q

<div class="cover">
  <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1934356646/ref=as_li_ss_il?ie=UTF8&tag=everrail-20&linkCode=as2&camp=217145&creative=399369&creativeASIN=1934356646"><img border="0" src="http://ws.assoc-amazon.com/widgets/q?_encoding=UTF8&Format=_SL110_&ASIN=1934356646&MarketPlace=US&ID=AsinImage&WS=1&tag=everrail-20&ServiceVersion=20070822" ></a><img src="http://www.assoc-amazon.com/e/ir?t=&l=as2&o=1&a=1934356646&camp=217145&creative=399369" width="1" height="1" border="0" alt="" style="border:none !important; margin:0px !important;" />
</div>

Noel Rappin's _Rails Test Prescriptions_ is my favorite book on the art and craft of test-driven development in Rails. Noel introduces you to popular testing frameworks for Ruby and Javascript, as well as associated concepts like stubs, mocks, and factories. If you're coming to Rails from an environment where automated testing was uncommon or unheard of, I strongly recommend picking up this book.
